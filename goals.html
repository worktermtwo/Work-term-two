Goals

My first Co-op term was spent working as an IT Support Staff, which was not exactly what I had pictured as a software engineering student. Therefore I
was incredibly excited when given the chance to work as a programmer in my second work term. I knew I would be developing hard skills through practice,
so I ensured that I also worked to improve some soft skills during my tenure with Hospitality services. I focused on 4 particular areas of personal development.

Oral/Written Communication: I worked in my own office, on a seperate floor than my supervisor. This meant that I was often working alone, without
supervision or oversight. I quickly understood the importance of autonomy, but still felt the need to ask questions and seek guidance from my superior.
This was difficult however, as he was often in meetings or working on projects, so I focused on communicating effectively, and established scheduled meetings.
Once I began meeting with him every monday morning to review my previous weeks work, and plan ahead, it became much easier to understand my project goals
and stay on track.

Learning Microsoft .NET: Hospitality Services builds all of their applications in a .NET framework, which I had little to no experience with before
this work term, so I quickly set a goal for myself to learn C#, ASP, VB and all other .NET tools as soon as possible so that I could contribute more to the
organization. Within 2 weeks I had taken on my first Razor C# web project, and by the end of the semester I was comfortable working on and creating .Net Apps.
These tools are very present in industry, so I am thankful for the oppurtunity.

Problem Solving: When you work as an application developer within Hospitality, problems and assignments are often left intentionally ambiguous. This is
because the culture of the office is to handle tasks and solve problems creatively in whichever way you feel is best. This can be much more stressful than following
strict requirements, but it also provides you with the oppurtunity to think critically. I soon learned that simply jumping into a solution for a problem before
thinking about it extensively was a recipe for disaster, and I began plotting out my plans before starting any projects. Once I began taking my time to think about
Issues, my solutions became far more sophisticated, succesful and consistent.

Understanding MSSQL and Oracle Databases: Hospitality Services uses a Oracle and MSSQL database backend for all systems and applications, which is something
I had not been given the chance to work with before this term. Understanding the imprortance of the dataa in these systems, and the privacy concerns involved was
crucial, as any mistake could be catastrophic for the department. I began watching hours of tutorials on Database querys and best practices in order to better handle
my everyday work, and as a result I did not need to rollback any database transactions or restore to a backup once during my term.
